Introduction

As chemistry is the study of matter, its properties and how it changes, it is imperative that we understand what matter is fundamentally made of.

Matter is all around you; it's what you see and touch, things that take up space and have mass. But matter, in whatever form it exists in, is made up of these tiny atoms which can be thought of as the building blocks for all matter. But contrary to what scientists and philosophers used to think when they first conceived this idea, atoms aren’t indivisible. Atoms have their own complex structure, made up of subatomic particles that are infinitely important in understanding chemistry.

Structure of the Atom

All atoms are composed of a nucleus at the centre of an atom and an electron cloud with one or more electrons that are bound to the nucleus. The nucleus is the dense, heavy centre of the atom with, and it is what gives virtually all of an atom’s mass (99.94%). The electron cloud on the other hand has virtually no mass but is what gives an atom most of its volume with a radius 10,000 times greater than that of the nucleus. In fact, an atom is pretty much just a dense heavy nucleus surrounded by a vast area of empty space with a few electrons littered here and there.

The Nucleus

The nucleus is made up of 2 basic subatomic particles being the neutron and proton. These two subatomic particles have approximately the same mass and also the same angular momentum or spin. The proton has a positive electric charge while the neutron has a negative electric charge. These two subatomic particles are held together by strong force and in much of high-school chemistry it is unlikely to be studying cases where the nucleus undergoes a fundamental chemical change such as splitting in two. However, the nucleus is still crucial in determining the properties of an atom.

This is firstly because the number of protons determines what element an atom is. The number of protons is called the atomic number and quite simply, this number is the thing that sets atoms apart as different elements. For example, all atoms that have 29 protons in its nucleus must be copper; there are no exceptions. If we somehow add or take away a proton from the nucleus, the atom changes from one element (with its own set of distinct chemical behaviours) to another. The periodic table is arranged using this metric, with the table ordering its elements in ascending order of atomic number.

The second reason why the nucleus is important is because the number of neutrons determine the isotope of that element. Isotopes are two or more types of the same element that have the same atomic number but differ in the number of neutrons. While isotopes generally have similar chemical properties, they have different atomic mases and physical properties. The thing about isotopes is that there aren’t an infinite number of them for one type of atom. Typically, there are only a few possible number of neutrons (typically similar to the atomic number) for the atom to be stable and not decay rapidly. For example, the main isotopes of carbon are carbon-12, carbon-13 and carbon-14, with neutron numbers being 6, 7 and 8 respectively (carbon having 6 protons). We also calculate the mass number of an atom through the nucleus by adding the number of protons with the number of neutrons in an atom. For example, the 12 in carbon-12 indicates the atom’s mass number, derived from 6 protons plus 6 neutrons.

Electrons

Electrons are the subatomic particles found outside the nucleus in the electron cloud and are much smaller and lighter than protons or neutrons. In fact, they’re 1800 times smaller than protons/neutrons and thus have little impact in calculating an atom’s mass. Electrons are also negatively charged and thus have an electrostatic attraction to the positively charged nucleus. Electrons are quite complex to understand and more can be found here but unlike the nucleus, electrons can be easily gained or lost by atoms. This is because, the electrostatic force keeping electrons attracted to the nucleus and within the atom is significantly weaker than the strong force holding the nucleus together. In fact, most of the chemistry you’ll be learning about has literally everything to do with the transferal of these tiny subatomic particles, called electrons, from one atom to another. It’s integral to bonds which make molecules and compounds possible as well as chemical reactions which are responsible for most of the physical changes observed in nature.

The Charge of Atoms and Ions

We know that the subatomic particles of atoms have their individual charges with protons being positive, neutrons, neutral and electrons, negative. What this means is that an atom can also have an overall charge, seeing as it’s made up of these individually charged particles. Quite simply, if the number of protons and electrons are the same, the atom has a neutral charge.

The neutrons don’t matter for calculating charge as they have neutral charge. However, if an atom has more or fewer electrons than protons, then the atom has an overall negative or positive charge and called an ‘ion’. As an electron is negatively charged, if they are more electrons than protons, the atom is a negatively charged ion and vice versa. A negatively charged ion is called an anion while a positively charged ion is a cation. Since, the number of protons cannot be easily changed, the most common way, neutral atoms become ions is by losing or gaining electrons. This electron transferral is particularly important when looking at bonds later. A neutral atom losing an electron causes it to become a cation while gaining an electron causes it to be an anion. The charge of atoms is important as its one of the key mechanisms through which matter interacts: electrostatic attraction and repulsion.
